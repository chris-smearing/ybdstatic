What to look for in a sponge

The main purpose of a sponge is scrubbing to remove food and debris. Most often, sponges are used to remove caked-on food from dishes, pans and utensils, but in determining the best sponge, we found that it needs to be useful in a wide variety of functions. For instance, sponges are also used to scrub stovetops, BBQs, remove soap and scum from bathroom fixtures and tiles and even to wash cars.

However, your best space-saving bet is to find one or two sponges that will accomplish a wide range of cleaning goals. Obviously, this makes versatility an extremely important factor when choosing a sponge.

A second important component of sponges is absorbency, because sponges are often used to soak up excess liquid from spills prior to cleaning with a solvent. You also want them to be able to hold soapy water and assist in removing debris.

Types of sponges

Different types of sponges are needed for different types of jobs. Knowing the type of sponge that works best for your particular cleaning project will save you time and energy. Here is the low-down, including pros and cons, of the different types of sponges.

Cellulose

These are the most common types of cleaning sponge; theyâ€™re inexpensive and absorbent. Made from wood pulp and highly porous, cellulose sponges hold water and soap for easy cleaning. However, they have a tendency to trap food particles that can be difficult to fully rinse clean and break down the life of the sponge as well as harbor bacteria.

Cellulose with abrasive material attached

These two-sided sponges combine the benefits of cellulose and scrubber for tough jobs like cleaning an oven or BBQ grill. The abrasive side comes in different levels of toughness and some can damage finishes.

Different brands use different colors to indicate abrasiveness level, so check the package to find the level of softness that works for your cleaning job. For everyday usage, a non-scratch low abrasive scrub side works best.

Microfiber sponges

With their soft texture, microfiber sponges can be used on any surface, which is why theyâ€™re highly recommended for washing cars and windows. The only downside is that they donâ€™t have the grittiness to effectively remove caked-on debris, but they work well for removing oil and dirt.

Natural sponges

Natural sea sponges grow on the oceanâ€™s floors and are 100-percent natural and earth-friendly. These sponges are recommended for projects like washing windows and cars, because they are soft and highly absorbent. They may be too soft to effectively remove caked-on food in the kitchen.

Dry sponges

These sponges are usually made from rubber and are great on surfaces that canâ€™t tolerate water such as lampshades and fabric window coverings. They are also commonly used to clean soot after a fire. Once the dry sponge is covered with dirt or dust, you slice that part of the sponge off for a fresh sponge to clean with.

How to clean a sponge

While sponges are a highly effective cleaning tool, the downside is that they can be an ideal breeding ground for dangerous bacteria. Organisms can become lodged in between the sponge, and in this moist environment with plenty of food, new bacteria can grow at the rate of once every 20 minutes.

Not something you want to think about it when youâ€™re using that same sponge on a later load of dishes.

In order to lower the risk of cross-contamination, we recommend that you clean your sponges daily.

There are a few different cleaning techniques. The best bet is to completely submerge the sponge in a bleach-and-water mixture. Make a disinfecting solution with one part bleach to nine parts water (about 14 ounces of bleach to about 14 cups of water) and soak the sponge for 30 seconds or longer. Store the excess solution in an empty plastic milk jug under the sink so you always have it on hand, since youâ€™ll want a fresh bowl of bleach solution for each session.

Microwaving your sponge is another means of disinfection. The sponge should be submerged in a bowl of water and then placed in the microwave. This can be more difficult as the water must be heated to a boil in order to kill bacteria.

After sanitizing with either method, wring the sponge out, and allow it to dry to prevent any new bacteria from growing. Cleaning your sponge on a regular basis will help reduce the risk of growing and spreading bacteria, but you should also replace it frequently. This is especially important with kitchen sponges. Odor indicates the presence of bacteria, so if it starts to have a smell, make sure to toss it out immediately.

In addition to cleaning your sponge and replacing it regularly, there are a few â€œdonâ€™tsâ€ to keep in mind. Remember that sponges canâ€™t do everything. Avoid wiping up meat juices from ground beef or poultry in order to prevent cross-contamination and food poisoning. In general, avoid cleaning countertops with sponges. Your best bet in these cases is paper towels or disinfectant wipes.
